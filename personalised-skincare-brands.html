Best personalised skincare brands offering custom-made solutions

Publish date: 2024-08-07

With an endless stream of new, ‘must-have’ products entering the skincare industry, it can be difficult to decipher what really works. Often a case of trial and error, many of us will experiment with various products and ingredients in the hope that, in time, we’ll see results. While this is sometimes the case, the process of elimination can be costly, time-consuming, and occasionally risky.

But what of a different way to shop for skincare, one that removes the guesswork? Enter the custom-made. Recently gaining traction, more and more consumers are buying into brands that offer personalised products formulated with your specific skin needs and goals in mind. “The public are starting to realise that one size does not fit all and that a personalised approach to skincare can yield superior results,” says Dr Emma Craythorne, consultant dermatologist and founder of Klira.

The concept makes sense. Instead of wasting money on unnecessary products, these forward-thinking brands will recommend specific formulations to address your specific needs (following an online skin assessment). “Different skin types require different ingredients and formulations,” confirms Dr Craythorne. “Like shoes or clothes, creams need to ‘fit the skin’ and some styles or, in the case of creams, formulations suit some skin types better than others.”

How do personalised skincare brands work?

To figure out what your skin needs, most brands will use a digital tool to perform a skin assessment online. Using questionnaires and/or images, the tool will be able to gather information not only about your skin, but also about your lifestyle to generate personalised recommendations.

At Klira, the process begins with an in-depth questionnaire covering medical history, lifestyle and skin type. “The reason why there are so many questions is that we’re essentially replacing an in-clinic appointment,” explains Dr Craythorne. Customers then upload photographs of their face before their ‘SkinSize’ is calculated – “this is our secret weapon for helping you understand the epigenetics and genetics of your skin.” A four-step routine is then devised, which includes a bespoke Klira product.

Custom skincare brands Dermatica and Skin + Me work in a similar way. Following short online questionnaires and image uploads, the expert dermatology teams will prescribe a plan. As with Klira, the bespoke products will contain prescription-strength ingredients (like tretinoin) that aren’t available over the counter. “There are a variety of strengths and ingredients used depending on the concerns of the individual customer,” explains Dr Ash Sharma, medical doctor for Dermatica.

These brands all work on a subscription model, meaning you pay a monthly fee (there are also options to pay quarterly or yearly) to receive regular top-ups of your bespoke formula. “The formulation and ingredient strengths will change each month to evolve with the skin’s tolerance,” says Dr Jason Thomson, dermatologist and head of medical at Skin + Me. “Customers can also check-in with an expert to discuss their progress at any time, free of charge.”

What are the benefits of personalised skincare?

“Personalised skincare gives people access to the right combinations of active ingredients to target their specific skin concerns, at concentrations that will work best for their skin,” says Dr Malvina Cunningham, consultant dermatologist at And Begin (Skin + Me’s sister brand). Rather than using generic off-the-shelf products, custom-made skincare ensures your specific skin concerns are targeted. “If there are certain ingredients your skin really needs, we put them in – and if there are ingredients you skin would do better without (like some preservatives or cosmeceuticals), we leave them out,” adds Dr Craythorne.

Custom-made also means it’s fresh. At Klira and Dermatica, the products are blended in London labs and delivered straight to the customers door within 48-hours so that the formulation is at its best with less degradation. “People want fresh products with minimal additional preservatives in,” notes Dr Craythorne, “they want ingredients that are at their peak potency and haven’t been stored on the shelf for months or years”.

Freshness aside, many personalised skin services offer access to a dermatology specialist for check-ins. “There are thousands of people across the country on the waiting list for a dermatology appointment,” flags Dr Sharma. “More than 8,000 people are currently waiting for an appointment in Scotland alone, and while those who can afford it go private, the prescription fees exclude the vast majority of people in the UK from accessing these services.” By offering online consultations, these brands have democratised access to seeing dermatology experts with treatment at affordable prices.

Are there any risks associated with personalised skincare?

“While I understand that not everyone can see a dermatologist, the current trend of virtual consultations and prescriptions has unfortunately led to a concerning rise of misdiagnosis,” says Dr Anjali Mahto, consultant dermatologist at Self London. “It’s not unusual for me to see four-to-six patients a week who have been diagnosed with acne by an online prescription service, but they actually have rosacea or vice versa.”

The point of having regular check-ins with a dermatologist specialist is to minimise these risks. “Our dermatology team will not treat any skin condition that they are not confident in diagnosing from the photographs provided,” says Dr Thomson. “And while there are always potential risks when applying treatments onto the skin – the benefit of personalisation means irritating ingredients, like retinoids and azelaic acid, can be added at lower strengths and gradually increased to reduce this risk.”

“The risks of bespoke personalised skincare are far less than the ‘DIY’ model,” says Dr Craythorne. In her practise, Dr Craythorne often sees patients who have made their skin worse by using the wrong products and/or incompatible ingredients. According to her, the main risk associated with personalised skincare is that the customer may think they have seen a dermatologist when they’ve actually seen a ‘skincare expert’ – which isn’t the same thing.

“I think it is important that you see a real dermatologist, or a team led by a dermatologist as that is how the skin nuances and diagnoses are detected and best plans are made,” she says.

The top personalised skincare brands in the business…

1. Dermatica

Noticing a need for dermatology-recommended skincare, health entrepreneur Dwayne D’Souza dreamt up Dermatica. The UK’s first online prescription-only skincare, Dermatica launched back in 2018 and has been a big success ever since (in the last two years, monthly subscribers doubled). Straightforward and fuss-free, “you complete an online consultation before our expert dermatology team prescribe your personalised formula,” explains Dr Sharma. These formulas can treat anything from acne and pigmentation to rosacea and fine lines with prescription-strength ingredients (such as tretinoin, benzoyl peroxide, ivermectin). Dermatica's personalised formula costs £24.99 per month.

2. Skin + Me

Landing on the personalised skincare scene in 2020, Skin + Me is another virtual service with the same goal – to democratise dermatology. The team of consultant dermatologists and pharmacists treat acne, pigmentation, rosacea and fine lines with custom-blended creams that contain prescription-only ingredients (like clindamycin and tretinoin) as well as other actives (such as niacinamide). Packaged in a recyclable aluminium tube, a monthly supply of the ‘Daily Doser’ costs £29.99 and to ensure no product goes to waste, the clever tube dispenses the perfect amount every time.

Courtesy of And Begin

3. And Begin

“As we studied the habits and feedback of hundreds of thousands of Skin + Me consumers, we realised that midlife women need more from their formulation,” says Dr Cunningham on the catalyst for creating And Begin (launching this year). “It became clear that this consumer wants the superior skin benefits of prescription ingredients in a personalised product that’s tailored to their life stage.” The process is similar – with an online consultation, followed by a prescribed serum that costs £39.99 per month – but the ingredients differ slightly, targeting firmness, brightness, even skin tone and hydration.

4. Get Harley

Not a personalised skincare brand per se, GetHarley is a platform that connects you with the UK’s top clinicians for expert skincare advice. To get started, you simply answer an online survey about your skin concerns/goals before being matched to a clinician (which could be an aesthetic doctor, plastic surgeons, dermatologist or expert facialist). You’ll then have the opportunity to discuss your skin – whether you need advice on a specific condition (such as rosacea or acne) or need help building a routine that’s suited to you. A 30-minute virtual consultation starts at £40.

5. Klira

Dr Craythorne saw a gap in the market for luxurious skincare with prescribed ingredients. “As a dermatologist, I knew I could develop formulas that were truly medical grade for those who could benefit.” During the pandemic, she developed an at-home assessment tool for patients and since then, Klira been able to offer these personalised bespoke formulas at scale. Costing £49 for a four-week supply, the custom formula – the ‘Klira special’ – contains a combination of soothing ingredients and prescription actives, tailored to your skin needs.

6. Uncouth

Another personalised skin service, Uncouth begins with a medical skin questionnaire and ends with a bi-monthly delivery of a custom-made cream (that costs £48.50). At its base, the cream contains a mixture of moisturising ingredients, including vitamin E and triglycerides, to support the skin’s barrier. The base is then added to with prescription-only actives to target individual concerns. As well as creams, Uncouth can also prescribe oral medications (where needed) from £15 per month.
